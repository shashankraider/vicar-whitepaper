Different leaves on purpose.

The "single productivity score" everyone asks for is a blur of distinct things forced into false unity. VICAR draws each dimension as its own leaf (a different colour, a different silhouette, a different length), so a leader stops staring at a meaningless average and starts seeing the structure.

VICAR Insight

A score ranks people; a shape coaches them. The moment a number can rank two engineers, both of them stop improving the work and start improving the number.

That difference is the point. A short Velocity leaf beside a long Complexity one is the quiet architect, not a slacker. Read the silhouette, and you can coach the person in front of you.

Each leaf carries its own colour as well as its own length, so the five dimensions never blur together. Read the silhouette and the palette at once, and the structure a single average would hide is right there on the page. Where a band genuinely lacks trustworthy data, the framework says "not enough signal" rather than inventing one, because a fabricated band is worse than a missing one.

Abstract

Developer productivity is not a number. Every attempt to compress it into a single score (a velocity figure, a commit count, an "AI usage" percentage) fails the same way: it measures motion instead of value, and the moment it becomes a target, it stops measuring anything true.

VICAR refuses the single number. Productivity is a multi-dimensional shape, read as a trend against a team's own baseline and used to start a coaching conversation. The five dimensions that carry the signal are Velocity (productive flow), Impact (whether the work mattered), Complexity (the difficulty and blast radius of what was built), AI adoption (read against leverage, not usage), and Rigor (engineering quality and hygiene). AI adoption is a core driver, not an afterthought; in an AI-era org, higher adoption tends to lift the other bands. The shape is read as a trend, used as the opening line of a conversation, and never as an input to evaluation.

i. why measuring productivity is broken

The default ways of answering "are the teams productive?" don't just fail; they corrode.

Five well-documented failures explain why any single number you can rank people on will be gamed by the smart people being ranked.

Goodhart's Law

When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. Tell people their commit count is what matters and you get more commits, each one smaller and emptier. Picking a "better" metric doesn't save you: any number breaks the same way once people know they're judged on it.

Output is not value

Lines, PRs and "velocity" measure how much motion there was, not how much progress. A 2,000-line pull request can be a liability the team pays down for a year; the quarter's most valuable change might be a four-line bug fix. Often, more output means less value, not more.

Activity is not productivity

Busy is not the same as effective. Commits, tickets closed and hours logged are tempting to track because they're easy to count and look objective, but looking busy is the easiest thing in the world to fake.

The surveillance trap

Good engineering runs on honesty: admitting "I'm stuck," and taking on hard work that won't show up on any chart. The moment people believe they're being scored as individuals, that honesty disappears: they play to the metric and hide the rest. And once trust is gone, you don't get it back.

AI adoption is not AI leverage

Seats, prompts, tokens, "percent of developers using the assistant" measure usage, not value. An org can hit 100% adoption and see zero leverage, while a usage dashboard happily reports it as success. Adoption without leverage is just spend with a progress bar. The throughline across all five: single metrics and individual rankings don't merely fail to measure productivity; they degrade it.

ii. the five leaves

The five bands that carry the signal, and what each means now AI is in the loop.

Five dimensions, read on their own AI-era terms. They form the name: VICAR.

leaf 01

VVelocity

Productive flow: throughput and cycle time, read as trend within cohort, not raw count.

What it reads

Is work moving, and is it moving faster or slower than this team's own normal? It answers velocity without worshipping it. Story points stay out, because the moment you score the estimate you teach people to inflate it.

Significance in the world of AI

AI inflates this band the easiest, which is exactly why it can never stand alone. A tall Velocity leaf is a question, not an answer: is this leverage, or churn the other four leaves will expose?

How it builds a high-performing team

It gives a shared, honest pulse of delivery and a baseline to trend against, so "slower than your own baseline" becomes a coaching signal, never "slower than the person next to you."

VICAR Insight

In the AI era, a sudden spike in Velocity is an orange flag, not a victory lap. If Velocity doubles while Rigor flatlines, your system isn't getting faster; it's just accumulating debt at machine speed.

leaf 02

IImpact

Was the work pointed at what matters: new value versus maintenance churn, criticality of the systems touched.

What it reads

It rewards the quiet, decisive four-line fix over the noisy two-thousand-line churn, and it reads that from system-of-record markers, never opinion or self-tagging: whether a change touches a tier-1 service rather than a tier-3 one, whether it modifies core business-logic APIs, and whether it links to an enterprise epic category the org already ranked as important. Those markers are auditable down to the event and hard to game, because the service tiers and the epic taxonomy are owned upstream, not by the engineer being read.

Significance in the world of AI

AI removes the friction of doing work, which makes direction the bottleneck. A team can now produce enormous, beautifully-tested output aimed at nothing that matters. Impact is the leaf that asks whether all that new velocity points at the right target.

How it builds a high-performing team

It connects individual effort to shared goals and exposes misalignment early. It is the leaf that asks, of every busy week, whether the motion was aimed at something the business actually decided was important.

leaf 03

CComplexity

The difficulty of the work: blast radius, design and research effort, the illegible thinking that produces few commits and one excellent decision.

What it reads

How hard the work actually was, measured from static-analysis signals, not a file count: cyclomatic-complexity trends (whether the branching difficulty of the code someone touches is rising or falling over time), architectural blast radius (how many downstream systems a change reaches), and deep code-path refactoring, alongside the design and research that lives in documents rather than commits. It is why VICAR does not mistake quiet for idle.

Significance in the world of AI

This is the leaf AI can't grow for you. Models accelerate the typing; they don't decide what to build, how it fits, or what breaks. As routine code gets cheaper, the scarce, defensible work shifts toward design, and Complexity is the only band that sees it.

How it builds a high-performing team

Crossed with Velocity it catches the quiet architect doing hard, low-PR-count work every activity metric would punish, so the team learns to value and protect that work instead of penalising it.

leaf 04

AAI Adoption

AI investment read against what it produced: the throughput and difficulty earned per token of spend, not usage counts.

What it reads

Leverage, not usage. True ROI puts output over investment, not the other way around:

AI Leverage= Velocity + ComplexityToken Spend

The numerator is what the engineer produced: shipped throughput plus the harder Complexity work (research, spikes, triage, design) that throughput alone never captures; the denominator is what it cost. Getting more throughput and more complexity out of fewer tokens is superior leverage; burning tokens for little movement is not. Adoption is the cost; leverage is the return, and only the return belongs on a productivity instrument.

Significance in the world of AI

The band the older thinking predates, promoted here to a first-class dimension rather than a footnote. It is framed as enablement, not a spend gotcha: find who gets leverage, who pays without payoff, and who hasn't started, so support can be targeted. Because output includes Complexity, the architect spending heavily on AI to do research and design reads as leverage, not as "not converting."

How it builds a high-performing team

It turns AI rollout from a hope into a read: a coaching prompt for who to pair, who to unblock, and where the spend is paying off, never a score folded into a number.

VICAR Insight

An organisation can reach 100% AI adoption and produce zero leverage. Usage is the receipt; leverage is the return. A dashboard that celebrates seats is measuring the bill, not the value.

leaf 05

RRigor

Engineering discipline as a leading signal: test-coverage trends, CI health, change-failure rate.

What it reads

Quality and hygiene read early, not after the incident. It is the band that keeps Velocity honest; it ensures speed is not being bought by mortgaging stability, the trade-off a single score would have hidden.

Significance in the world of AI

When code is generated faster than it's read, rigor is the guardrail against confidently-wrong output flooding the repo. The risk isn't laziness; it's volume. This leaf asks the question AI tempts everyone to skip: was the generated code actually verified?

How it builds a high-performing team

It turns "we should test more" into specific, auditable evidence. Because it's gameable on its own, it stays diagnostic; it corroborates the other leaves rather than raising a flag alone.

iii. shape over score

A uniform radar hides people. Differentiated leaves reveal them.

The same five readings can be drawn two ways. Only one lets a manager see the engineer.

The single-score view

One grey blob. Every dimension reads the same; the trade-offs that are the whole point are hidden; the engineer is indistinguishable from the next.

The VICAR view

A legible signature. Long Velocity, modest Complexity, each dimension its own colour: a person you can actually coach.

Because the leaves differ, you compare shapes, not totals, and only within a like work-type cohort. A cohort is a domain topology, not a job title: platform engineering, greenfield product-feature teams, and legacy-maintenance teams are three different cohorts, because the work itself has a different shape. A staff architect on a legacy migration and a new-grad on a greenfield service are not on the same axis. Read as a trend, "down thirty percent against their own trailing average" is a reason to ask a kind question; "scored 0.6" is a reason for someone to feel judged and for no one to learn anything.

Shapes you will learn to recognise

Once you read shape instead of score, a handful of silhouettes start to repeat. None is a verdict; each is the opening line of a different conversation.

The Ghost Architect

A sliver of Velocity, towering Complexity and Impact. The quiet senior who ships a four-line architectural fix that saves the quarter.

A score-based system buries this person; their Velocity looks "low." The shape says protect and amplify them, and never let a velocity dashboard penalise the work that mattered most.

The AI Firehose

Huge Velocity and AI adoption, collapsed Rigor and Impact. The developer shipping LLM output at volume without reading it, generating churn and debt.

On any single metric this looks like a top performer. The shrunken Rigor and Impact leaves are the tell: the conversation is about review and direction, not speed.

The Maintenance Anchor

High Rigor, flat Velocity, low Impact. The engineer polishing a legacy system the business has quietly stopped betting on.

This shape is rarely about the person; it is about the assignment. Read in cohort context, it is a prompt to question whether the work still matters, or to move them onto something with headroom.

The Leverage Engine

Full and balanced, AI adoption converting into both Velocity and Complexity while Impact and Rigor hold.

This is the shape to study and spread: AI investment that actually turns into output and harder problems solved. The template for what good looks like on an AI-era team.

Read your own shape in 5 minutes
  1. Pick a cohort, not a person. Choose one like-work team (platform, greenfield, or legacy maintenance). Never start with an individual.
  2. Grab two honest bands. Pull the two you already trust, usually Velocity (cycle-time trend) and Rigor (change-failure rate). Skip anything you would have to guess.
  3. Plot the trend, not the total. For each band ask one thing: up, flat, or down against this team's own last few months? Direction beats the absolute number.
  4. Read the silhouette. Is one leaf racing ahead of the rest? A long Velocity leaf beside a short Rigor leaf is a Firehose; a short Velocity leaf beside a long Complexity leaf is a Ghost Architect.
  5. Ask the question, don't assign the score. Turn the lopsided leaf into one curious question: "Velocity is up but Rigor is flat: are we trading stability for speed?" That question is the entire deliverable.

If at any point you reached for a ranking, start over. The output is a conversation, never a verdict.

iv. applying vicar

Adopt the stance before the dashboard. Add resolution over time, never teeth.

The failure mode of every productivity initiative is starting at the individual and starting with a score. VICAR inverts both. The staged path is a shield, not bureaucracy: each stage earns the next, so the framework never outruns the data or the trust beneath it. Rush it and you manufacture the very chaos VICAR exists to prevent, half-measured bands brandished as a scoreboard.

stage 1

Establishing the baseline

Stop guessing.

Begin at the team, never the individual. Take the two bands you can measure honestly from systems you already trust (usually Velocity and Rigor) and read them against the team's own baseline. You stop arguing from anecdote and start reading from the team's own trend.

VR

stage 2

Layering context

Stop measuring churn.

Layer in Impact once you can tell new-value work from maintenance, and Complexity once you can characterise difficulty and blast radius. Now a busy week that shipped nothing that mattered stops reading as productivity. Each band added honestly beats three you fake.

IC

stage 3

Maximising AI leverage

The upstream engine.

AI adoption is not a final add-on; it is the upstream driver of the whole shape. Engineers with higher adoption tend to ship more (Velocity) and solve harder problems (Complexity), so read it as the leading signal that explains movement in the other bands. It follows V and C only because leverage divides their output by token spend: (Velocity + Complexity) ÷ tokens, never raw usage.

A

Throughout: coaching-first and cohort-bounded. The read surfaces where to look and what conversation to start; comparison is bounded to like cohorts and gated behind a deliberate action; the output reaches a manager as a prompt to help, never an HR system, never a comp input, never a ranking. The maturity path adds resolution to the shape; it never adds teeth. The day VICAR grows teeth is the day it stops measuring anything true.

The framework is the principles

Get these seven right and the specific metrics almost don't matter. Get them wrong and the best metrics will mislead you.

  1. Multi-dimensional, never a single score.Track a shape across dimensions. The refusal of the single number is the foundation, not a feature.
  2. Coaching, not evaluation.Measure to find where to help. The output is a conversation, never a rating, comp, or HR input.
  3. Trend over snapshot.Movement against one's own baseline is signal; an absolute number compared across people is noise.
  4. Auditable signals over opinions and LOC.Every signal traces to evidence in a system of record (a service tier, an API path, a cyclomatic-complexity trend, a dependency graph) and answers "why?" down to the event. No opinions, no self-reported tags to game.
  5. Outcomes over activity.Anchor on whether the work mattered (value shipped, criticality touched), not the volume of motion.
  6. AI adoption is a core driver.In an AI-era org, higher adoption tends to lift Velocity and the Complexity a person can solve. Treat it as a first-class, leading dimension, measured as leverage: output and difficulty per token of spend, (Velocity + Complexity) ÷ tokens, never raw usage.
  7. Compare only within like cohorts.Compare shapes within a work-type cohort, defined by domain topology rather than job title (platform vs. greenfield feature vs. legacy maintenance). Comparison sits behind a deliberate action; never rank totals across cohorts.

VICAR is not a verdict on people. It is a better question to ask about a system, and a better place to start a conversation.

appendix · for the record

VICAR and the state of the art: a practitioner's map.

VICAR doesn't replace the established frameworks; it makes their multidimensional discipline practical as a coaching instrument for AI-era development. Here's how it sits against the prior art, and what changes once AI is writing much of the code.

DORA (2018). Four keys (deployment frequency, lead time, change-failure rate, time to restore) that measure speed and stability together at the system level. That still matters with AI in the loop: AI inflates commit and PR volume, and DORA's stability-paired view guards against shipping fast and breaking things. But it reads the delivery pipeline, not people, difficulty, or whether the work mattered. VICAR borrows its discipline for Velocity and Rigor.

SPACE (2021). The meta-framework: productivity is multidimensional, so never measure on one axis. This is more vital, not less, once AI makes a single number like Velocity trivial to inflate. But SPACE is a way of thinking, not an operating model; it names the shape of a good measurement system, not which signals to put on the wall Monday morning. VICAR is one concrete instantiation of that discipline.

DevEx (2023). Reframes productivity through lived experience: feedback loops, cognitive load, flow state. AI tools reshape all three: faster feedback, but a new cognitive load in reviewing generated code you didn't write. DevEx surfaces drag before it shows up in delivery, but it leans on perception and describes the conditions for productivity more than the work itself. In VICAR it is the "why" behind a slow Velocity or Rigor band.

DX Core 4 (2024). The synthesis built for the AI era: Speed, Effectiveness, Quality, Impact, with developer experience as a leading indicator. It is the right operating model at leader altitude. What it doesn't prescribe is how to keep the read coaching-first and cohort-fair once it reaches a line manager and an individual, and it treats AI as context rather than a first-class, measured driver. VICAR brings that discipline down to the coaching conversation, with AI adoption promoted to a core band.

VICAR is not a rejection of DORA, SPACE, DevEx or DX Core 4. It is their discipline made practical for the team actually shipping with AI.
